Change Management as Practice

Simple, fundamental messages win when we're purposeful about finding common interest and learning to vibe with contextual background noise.

8 min read Published February 2025 Change Management

Change management isn't about perfecting process—it's about making things work better for actual people. Too often, change initiatives fail because they prioritize adherence to methodology over outcomes.

"Outcomes and relationships trump process and adherence. The goal isn't perfect methodology—it's making things work better for actual people."

The Myth of Perfect Process

I've seen Fortune 500 companies spend months crafting the "perfect" change management framework, only to watch it crumble when it meets the reality of how people actually work. The truth is that sustainable transformation happens when you solve human problems, not just business problems.

In my 15+ years working with companies like USAA, Progressive, and MVP Healthcare, the most successful transformations happened when we started with shared problems, not solutions. When we listened to resistance as valuable data rather than obstacles to overcome.

Finding Common Ground

The best change agents I know don't try to eliminate organizational background noise—they learn to work with it. Every organization has contextual dynamics: unspoken tensions, historical baggage, informal power structures that influence how change really happens.

  • Start with shared problems, not solutions
  • Listen to resistance as valuable data
  • Adapt methodology to organizational culture
  • Measure relationship health alongside deliverables

Recognizing the Patterns

Learn to read the signals: the eye-roll during all-hands meetings translates to "here we go again." The "sounds great, but..." responses mean "I've seen this before." The sudden enthusiasm for old processes signals "I'm scared of losing what I know."

Case Study: Design System Adoption at USAA

When we needed to get 50+ development teams to adopt a new mobile design system, the traditional approach would have been to build perfect documentation, mandate adoption, and measure compliance.

What we did instead:

  • Found the team with the biggest pain point our system could solve
  • Solved their specific problem first
  • Let them become advocates to peer teams
  • Built adoption through peer influence rather than mandates

Result: 80% adoption in 6 months vs. the projected 18-month rollout through traditional change management.

The Win-Win Imperative

Every change initiative creates winners and losers. The art is in finding compromises that feel like wins for everyone involved—even when they're not perfect solutions. This requires asking different questions:

  • What does success look like for each stakeholder?
  • Where are the natural alignment points?
  • What fears need to be addressed before moving forward?
  • How can we make the transition feel collaborative, not imposed?

Beyond Process: Building Practice

Process is what you do when everything goes according to plan. Practice is how you respond when it doesn't.

The best change agents develop practices that can adapt to context while maintaining integrity of purpose. This means holding outcomes lightly while staying committed to direction, reading organizational energy and adjusting timing accordingly.

The Michigan Grit Approach

Growing up poor and quasi-feral in Kalamazoo taught me that survival requires resourcefulness over resources, relationships over rules, persistence over perfection. This translates directly to change work: the goal isn't elegant methodology—it's sustainable transformation that sticks.

Practical Change Management

Change management works best as a practice, not a project. Like jazz improvisation, it requires strong fundamentals, deep listening, and responsive adaptation.

Reading Organizational Energy

  • Observe informal communication patterns
  • Notice what gets discussed in hallway conversations
  • Track which initiatives get enthusiasm vs. compliance
  • Identify natural coalition builders and resisters

Building Sustainable Change

  • Start with problems that people already feel
  • Create quick wins that demonstrate new possibilities
  • Build advocacy through peer influence
  • Establish feedback loops for continuous adaptation

Measuring What Matters

  • Track relationship health alongside deliverables
  • Monitor cross-team collaboration instances
  • Measure advocacy (teams recommending to other teams)
  • Assess system resilience under pressure

The Poetry of Change

There's a rhythm to organizational change that's more like Latin jazz than classical music. It's polyrhythmic—multiple patterns happening simultaneously, sometimes in tension, sometimes in harmony.

Successful change management means learning to conduct this orchestra without losing the essential human elements that make the music worth playing. It means finding the groove between structure and improvisation, between direction and discovery.

"Simple, fundamental messages win when we're purposeful about finding common interest and learning to vibe with contextual background noise."
